The joy of multisection

6 July 2026 · ~5 min

Here is a question that sounds harder than it is: given a power series, how do you keep only the terms whose exponents lie in a fixed arithmetic progression? Say you have $f(x) = \sum_{k \ge 0} a_k x^k$ and you only want the terms where $k$ is even, or $k \equiv 2 \pmod 5$. The answer is a single, endlessly reusable trick, and it's genuinely one of my favourite things in elementary analysis.

Warm-up: even and odd parts

Start with the simplest case, $n = 2$. Notice that replacing $x$ with $-x$ flips the sign of exactly the odd-powered terms. So adding $f(x)$ and $f(-x)$ cancels everything odd and doubles everything even:

$$\frac{f(x) + f(-x)}{2} = \sum_{k \text{ even}} a_k x^k.$$

Apply this to $e^x$ and you recover a classic:

$$\frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2} = \cosh x = \sum_{k \ge 0} \frac{x^{2k}}{(2k)!}.$$

The subtraction gives you $\sinh x$ and the odd terms. Nothing surprising yet — but the mechanism generalises beautifully.

The roots-of-unity filter

For a general modulus $n$, the sign flip $x \mapsto -x$ becomes a rotation by an $n$-th root of unity. Let $\omega = e^{2\pi i / n}$. The claim is that

$$\frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \omega^{-jr} \, f(\omega^j x) \;=\; \sum_{k \equiv r \,(\mathrm{mod}\ n)} a_k \, x^k.$$

Why does it work? The whole thing hinges on one fact about roots of unity: summing $\omega^{jm}$ over a full period of $j$ gives $n$ when $n \mid m$ and $0$ otherwise. Plug $f(\omega^j x) = \sum_k a_k \omega^{jk} x^k$ into the left side, swap the order of summation, and the inner sum over $j$ acts as a detector — it keeps exactly the coefficients with $k \equiv r$ and kills the rest. That's the entire idea.

Multisection turns a question about coefficients into a question about symmetry: which rotations leave the terms you care about fixed?

A worked example

Let's do something concrete. What is the sum of every third binomial coefficient,

$$\binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{3} + \binom{n}{6} + \cdots \;=\; \sum_{k \equiv 0 \,(\mathrm{mod}\ 3)} \binom{n}{k}\,?$$

Take $f(x) = (1+x)^n = \sum_k \binom{n}{k} x^k$ and filter at $r = 0$, $n = 3$, with $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. Evaluating at $x = 1$:

$$\sum_{k \equiv 0 \,(\mathrm{mod}\ 3)} \binom{n}{k} = \frac{1}{3}\left[ 2^n + (1+\omega)^n + (1+\omega^2)^n \right].$$

The pretty part is what $1 + \omega$ actually is. Since $\omega = -\tfrac12 + \tfrac{\sqrt3}{2} i$, we get $1 + \omega = \tfrac12 + \tfrac{\sqrt3}{2} i = e^{i\pi/3}$, a unit-modulus number. Its conjugate is $1 + \omega^2 = e^{-i\pi/3}$, so the two stray terms combine into a cosine:

$$\sum_{k \equiv 0 \,(\mathrm{mod}\ 3)} \binom{n}{k} = \frac{1}{3}\left( 2^n + 2\cos\frac{n\pi}{3} \right).$$

A clean closed form, and the small oscillating correction term is exactly the fingerprint of the two non-trivial roots of unity. Quick sanity check at $n = 3$: the left side is $\binom{3}{0} + \binom{3}{3} = 2$, and the right side is $\tfrac13(8 + 2\cos\pi) = \tfrac13(8 - 2) = 2$. It checks out.

Why I keep coming back to it

What I love is how little machinery this needs. No contour integrals, no generating-function heavy lifting — just the orthogonality of roots of unity, applied with a bit of nerve. The same filter shows up when you split zeta values, extract lacunary series, or chase Bernoulli-number identities, which is more or less how it wandered into my own work. Once you see it, you start noticing places it wants to be used.
